The **introduction** and the **motivation** chapters serve different but complementary purposes in a thesis.
They answer different questions for the reader:

---

## **1. Introduction**

**Main purpose:**
Give the reader an *overview* of the topic and set the *context* for the work.
It tells the reader **what the thesis is about** and **how it fits into a broader field**.

**Answers questions like:**

* *What is the topic?*
* *What is the general problem area?*
* *Why is this area relevant right now?*
* *What will this thesis cover at a high level?*
* *How is the document structured?*

**Typical content in an Introduction:**

1. **Context / Background** ‚Äì High-level explanation of the field (e.g., XR in industry, human‚Äìmachine interaction, safety systems).
2. **Problem domain** ‚Äì Where the work is situated (e.g., industrial safety and ergonomics).
3. **Scope of the work** ‚Äì What this thesis will address and what it won‚Äôt.
4. **Research gap** ‚Äì A hint at what‚Äôs missing in current approaches.
5. **High-level objectives** ‚Äì Broad goals (not the detailed task list yet).
6. **Thesis structure** ‚Äì Short paragraph explaining what‚Äôs in each chapter.

> **Rule of thumb:** The introduction is like the ‚Äúexecutive summary‚Äù of the research context and scope.

---

## **2. Motivation**

**Main purpose:**
Convince the reader *why the work matters* and *why it should be done now*.
It justifies the research by showing **why the problem is worth solving**.

**Answers questions like:**

* *Why is this problem important?*
* *What is the impact if we don‚Äôt solve it?*
* *Who benefits from solving it?*
* *Why is now the right time to address it?*
* *Why is your approach a good fit for solving it?*

**Typical content in a Motivation section:**

1. **Practical relevance** ‚Äì Industrial, economic, or societal needs.
2. **Limitations of current solutions** ‚Äì Why existing methods don‚Äôt fully solve the problem (e.g., static safety protocols fail in dynamic factory environments).
3. **Opportunity for improvement** ‚Äì How XR, wearables, and your framework could do better.
4. **Impact** ‚Äì Expected benefits (e.g., fewer injuries, improved productivity, better ergonomic compliance).
5. **Novelty** ‚Äì What makes your work different from previous approaches.

> **Rule of thumb:** Motivation is the ‚Äúsales pitch‚Äù for your research ‚Äî it should make the reader feel that this work *must* be done.

| Aspect             | **Introduction**                    | **Motivation**                             |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| **Focus**          | Context & scope                     | Importance & urgency                       |
| **Tone**           | Informative                         | Persuasive                                 |
| **Answers**        | *What is this about?*               | *Why does this matter?*                    |
| **When read**      | To understand the field and project | To be convinced the project is worth doing |
